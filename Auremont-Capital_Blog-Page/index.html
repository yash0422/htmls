GCC Capital Flows 2025:
Where Family Offices Are Deploying

VD
Vishal Desai
Founder & Managing Director, Auremont Capital
April 2025Published
9 minRead time
GCC OutlookCategory

The family offices I speak with daily aren’t asking “should we invest in real estate?”

They’re asking: which asset, which market, which structure — and how fast can we close?

In the past 90 days, I’ve had direct conversations with principals across 14 family offices in the GCC.

The pattern is unmistakable.

Capital is rotating. Not out of the region — deeper into it. And the asset preferences, ticket sizes, and deal structures have shifted in ways that most advisors haven’t caught up with yet.

The shift

What the data and
conversations reveal

Three forces are driving this rotation simultaneously: the residual volatility shock from 2023–24 equity markets, GCC governments accelerating their infrastructure and economic diversification mandates, and a widening yield gap between physical assets and fixed income in a normalising rate environment.

The result is a very specific type of capital — informed, patient, and selective — looking for quality at institutional scale.

Key signals

Five patterns I’m seeing
on the ground

→
Physical assets over liquid positions. The equity market volatility of 2023–24 left a lasting mark. Family offices are building positions in income-generating real estate, education assets, and infrastructure plays — not because equities are bad, but because the risk-return equation on physical GCC assets is simply compelling right now.
→
Education and healthcare are the new defensive alpha. 6–8% NNN yields with regulatory moats. KHDA and MOH frameworks provide the kind of rent protection that residential portfolios can’t match. I’m seeing first-time education asset buyers from families that have never considered this sector before.
→
Ticket sizes are consolidating upward. 18 months ago, AED 50–80M mandates were common. Today, the floor has shifted to AED 100M+. Family offices are writing fewer, bigger cheques — signalling conviction, not caution.
→
Off-market or nothing. If an asset has been on the market for more than 30 days, the working assumption is either the price is wrong or the asset is wrong. Deal quality is the gating factor, not capital availability. This is where advisors with genuine proprietary flow have structural advantage.
→
Geography is expanding. Dubai-first has evolved to GCC-wide, with selective positions in Sub-Saharan Africa and South Asia where yield premiums justify the complexity. Rwanda, Kenya, and India are appearing in conversations they weren’t 18 months ago.
Family offices aren’t short on capital. They’re short on quality. That distinction is everything for how you position an asset.
Implication

What this means for
asset owners

If you own an asset — or are preparing one for sale — your pricing strategy, documentation quality, and counterparty credibility matter more than at any point in the last decade.

The buyers are there. They are capitalized. They have clear mandates. What they don’t have patience for is ambiguity: in pricing, in title, in regulatory status, in operator quality.

The implication for sellers and developers is straightforward: prepare your asset for institutional scrutiny, not retail presentation. Detailed financial models, clean regulatory documentation, and a credible advisor relationship are table stakes.

For investors: if you’re not already seeing off-market deal flow in the AED 100M–500M range from advisors with real principal relationships, that’s the gap to address. The window on the current pricing environment won’t stay open.

Closing thought

Where I see this
heading

The GCC will not slow its physical asset accumulation. The diversification mandates — at the sovereign, institutional, and family office level — are structural, not cyclical. This is a decade-long rotation, not a 12-month trade.

The advisors and asset owners who will win in this environment are those who show up with quality, speed, and precision. Capital is not the constraint. It never was.
