Most people track "the oil price."
Professionals track which oil price — and why the spread between them matters.

There are two benchmarks at the core of every energy trade:

Brent Crude (Futures) — the forward-looking price. Traded on the ICE exchange, it reflects where the market expects oil to be. The global reference for ~80% of internationally traded crude. Majors like Shell, BP, and TotalEnergies use it as their primary pricing anchor for term contracts.

Dated Brent (Physical/Spot) — the real-world price. What an actual cargo of North Sea crude sells for today, on a specific delivery date. No speculation. Just real barrels, real transactions. It's what Saudi Aramco, ADNOC, and QatarEnergy reference when pricing physical liftings to Asia and Europe.

The spread between the two is a live signal — of supply tightness, demand shifts, geopolitical risk, and physical market stress.

When Dated Brent trades above futures → physical supply is tight. Buyers are competing for real barrels. Refiners — from ExxonMobil to Sinopec — are scrambling for prompt delivery.

When it trades below → the market is long. Supply is comfortable, or demand is softening.

Right now, that spread has blown out to historic levels. The closure of the Strait of Hormuz has removed ~14 million bpd from global supply. Dated Brent surged past $130/bbl in early April while futures lagged — a signal the paper market is failing to capture the true severity of the physical supply shock.

For institutional allocators and family offices positioning across energy-linked infrastructure, this isn't a technicality.

It's the most important risk signal in global markets right now.

Understanding the difference between paper oil and physical oil is the difference between reading headlines and reading markets.
